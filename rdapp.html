RD App UX Case

Project Overview


Drug use happens in a vacuum of misinformation. In Brazil, where the topic remains heavily stigmatized and criminalized, people who use substances often lack access to reliable, non-judgmental information that could prevent serious harm — or save their lives. App RD is a mobile harm reduction application being relaunched to provide evidence-based guidance on substance use, risk reduction strategies, and access to health services. This project is currently in the research and definition phase.

Role: Program Coordinator, UX Researcher

Team: General Coordinator, Developer, Design Team (3), Content Team (3)

Status: 🔄 Research & Definition Phase — 2026

Type: Volunteer project

The Problem


Substance use is not going away — but the harm that comes with it is largely preventable. The biggest barriers are not clinical. They are informational, social, and structural: people don't know their rights when approached by police, don't know how to identify a bad reaction, don't know what to do when someone overdoses in front of them, and don't know where to find help without being judged. The original App RD existed to address this gap. The relaunch exists to do it better — with stronger research foundations, more inclusive design, and a deeper understanding of who actually uses substances in Brazil.

Research & Insights


The relaunch began with something most apps skip: listening to the people who already used the first version.

Primary research - 30 users of the original app

A survey with 30 participants mapped both user profiles and their relationship with substance use. Key findings:

  • The audience is broader than assumed — Users ranged from university students to people in street situations, from young adults exploring substances for the first time to middle-aged professionals managing long-term use. A one-size-fits-all approach would fail most of them.
  • The most used substances are legal ones - Alcohol (25), cannabis (19), and tobacco (15) dominated the last-30-days use — meaning harm reduction for this audience is not primarily about illicit drugs. It's about the substances society already normalizes.
  • The biggest perceived problem is not health — it's prohibition. When asked about the worst thing that could happen when buying their substance of choice, 14 users cited being arrested or detained. Only 6 cited health concerns. The app must address the legal and social context of use, not just the pharmacological one.
  • Satisfaction with the original app was high but not enough — 29 out of 30 users rated the app as satisfactory or very satisfactory — confirming the concept works. The relaunch is about depth, accessibility, and reach, not reinvention

Define


Personas: Four users grounded in the data — not assumptions:

  • André, 27 — Black man, cannabis user from Zona Leste, São Paulo. Has experienced police violence and a bad trip. Wears glasses due to astigmatism and struggles with bright interfaces. Needs: harm reduction strategies, rights information, dark mode.
  • Isa, 23— White university student in Florianópolis, alcohol and LSD user, frequent rave attendee. Has experienced physical distress at an event. No internet access at events. Needs: offline functionality, emergency guidance, rave-specific harm reduction.
  • Jéssica, 37 — Trans woman, homeless, alcohol and cocaine user with hypertension. Has been arrested for possession and witnessed an overdose. Treats borderline disorder at CAPS AD. Low tolerance for long texts. Needs: fast, objective information, low reading demand, no stigma.
  • Luca, 19— Non-binary, cannabis and alcohol user exploring new substances. Lives with parents in rural Minas Gerais, has ADHD and depression, family conflicts around use. Needs: beginner-friendly content, information about drug interactions, non-judgmental tone.
  • Carlo, 40 — Uruguayan man, alcohol and tobacco user with recurring lung problems. Travels frequently for work. Speaks only Spanish. Needs: multilingual support, tobacco harm reduction, respiratory health information.

What Makes This Project Different



Most health apps are designed for people who already have access — to healthcare, to literacy, to stable internet, to safety. This project starts from the opposite assumption. Jéssica doesn't have time for long onboarding flows. Isa needs the app to work in a field with no signal. André needs content that acknowledges police violence as a health issue. Carlo can't use an app that only speaks Portuguese. Designing for these five users simultaneously is the central design challenge of App RD — and it's exactly the kind of problem that requires UX thinking grounded in equity, accessibility, and deep human understanding.
